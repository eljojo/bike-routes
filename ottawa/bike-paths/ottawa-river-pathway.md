The longest and most-used cycling corridor in the capital — and the oldest. The Ottawa River Pathway was the first section of the NCC's Capital Pathway network, built in the early 1970s. From the Rideau Falls in the east to Britannia Beach in the west, the pathway follows the south shore of the Ottawa River for roughly 30 kilometres — flat, paved, and open to the water almost the entire way.

The eastern stretch passes through the political heart of the city. Parliament Hill rises above you, the Supreme Court sits quietly to your left, and the Ottawa Locks — where the Rideau Canal meets the river — mark a natural pause point. Past the War Museum, the path opens up into longer, quieter sections through Remic Rapids Park, where great blue herons stand in the shallows and the sunset views are some of the best in the city.

Further west, the pathway passes Westboro Beach and its new pavilion, then continues through to Britannia, where The Beachconers serves ice cream that justifies the ride on its own. On weekends and holiday Mondays from May to October, the NCC closes the westbound lanes of the adjacent Kichi Zībī Mīkan — "Great River Road" in Algonquin, formerly the Sir John A. Macdonald Parkway — to cars for Weekend Bikedays, giving you 9 kilometres of parkway from Vimy Place to Carling Avenue as well.

This is the path that connects almost everything. The canal pathways branch south from here, the Trans Canada Trail overlaps in sections, and four bridges link you to the Gatineau side — including the Chief William Commanda Bridge, a converted rail crossing for people on foot and on bikes. The pathway is one thread in the NCC's Capital Pathway network, over 200 kilometres of multi-use paths that stretch from Gatineau Park through the urban core and into the Greenbelt. If you're new to Ottawa cycling, this is where you start.
