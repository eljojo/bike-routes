A [72-kilometre trail](https://www.sentierprtrail.com/en/about) linking Ottawa's eastern edge to the rural communities of Prescott and Russell counties, running from Hammond in the west to Saint-Eugène at the Quebec border in the east. Mostly stone dust with short paved sections near the towns. Part of the Trans Canada Trail.

The trail follows an old Canadian Pacific rail corridor that once connected Montreal to Ottawa — the M&O (Montreal & Ottawa) Subdivision. The rails were lifted in the 1980s, and the trail was [developed starting in 2002](https://www.sentierprtrail.com/en/about) — inspired by Quebec's P'tit Train du Nord. The first 54 km opened in June 2004, with the remaining 18 km completed the following year. Because it's a former railbed, it's flat and straight, with farmland on both sides and the occasional small town for water and snacks.

[Pavilions are spaced along the route](https://www.sentierprtrail.com/en/services) in Saint-Eugène, Vankleek Hill, Plantagenet, Bourget, and Hammond — good spots to refill water or take a break. The trail passes through the townships of East Hawkesbury, Champlain, The Nation, Alfred and Plantagenet, and Clarence-Rockland. It's a different kind of riding from the urban pathways: open sky, long sightlines, and the feeling of leaving the city behind.

The 26-km Prescott-Russell Trail Link continues west from Hammond into Ottawa, maintained by the City of Ottawa. The trail itself is operated by a non-profit, the Sentier récréatif Prescott-Russell Recreational Trail Corporation.

### Beyond Saint-Eugène — the corridor to Montreal

The same M&O railway corridor continues east across the Quebec border through Rigaud, Hudson, and Vaudreuil-Dorion. People ride it — the full Ottawa-to-Montreal trip is roughly 200 km on the south shore — but the Quebec section is not a maintained trail. Expect variable surfaces: some rideable gravel, some rough sections, some stretches where the old railbed hasn't been formally converted. Ville de Rigaud is actively working on converting their section into a proper paved path, but as of 2025 this is still in progress.

From Vaudreuil-Dorion, cycling infrastructure improves and you can follow established routes through the West Island into Montreal.

If you're considering the full corridor: bring wider tires, be prepared to detour onto roads in places, and treat everything past Saint-Eugène as an adventure rather than a groomed trail. The railway corridor is there — it's just not all the way there yet.
