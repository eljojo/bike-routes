Ottawa's Greenbelt is a 20,350-hectare ring of green space that encircles the urban core — the [largest publicly owned greenbelt in the world](https://ncc-ccn.gc.ca/places/greenbelt). It was proposed by French planner [Jacques Gréber in 1950](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenbelt_(Ottawa)) as part of his master plan for the capital, with the federal government acquiring land starting in 1956. Two pathway systems run through it — one on the western side, one on the eastern — both managed by the NCC and forming part of the Trans Canada Trail.

Not lit, so plan for daylight. Tires 32mm or wider are comfortable on both sections.

### Western pathway

The western arm runs 21.2 kilometres through protected forest, wetlands, and farmland. The surface alternates between asphalt and stone dust, mostly flat with some hills. It stretches from Shirleys Bay through forests, fields, and farm landscapes, passing near the Stony Swamp conservation area — the largest forested area within the Greenbelt, covering 20 square kilometres of provincially significant wetland. Meadows open up unexpectedly, creeks cross the path, and there are long stretches where the only sound is your tires on the gravel.

The western pathway connects to the Watts Creek Pathway, which links onward to the Kichi Zībī Mīkan along the Ottawa River.

### Eastern pathway

The eastern arm runs 12.5 km through protected forest and wetland on fine gravel. Two metres wide — narrower than the western section — and connects to the Ottawa River Pathway at its northern end. Quieter and less travelled, with a wilder feel.

The [Mer Bleue Bog](https://ncc-ccn.gc.ca/places/mer-bleue) is the highlight of this section — a roughly 7,700-year-old peat bog and the largest in the National Capital Region. The 3,500-hectare conservation area was designated a [Ramsar Wetland of International Significance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mer_Bleue_Bog) in 1995 and hosts a permanent research station, making it one of the most studied bogs in the world. Peat deposits here reach up to 6 metres thick. The name means "blue sea" in French, from the fog that settles over the bog in the morning. A 1.2 km boardwalk trail leads into the bog itself — worth locking up the bike for.
