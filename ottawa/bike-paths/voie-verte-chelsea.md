A former railway line converted into a 22 km gravel trail running along the Gatineau River from Gatineau through Chelsea toward Wakefield. Flat, mostly by the river, and stunning in autumn — this is one of the most popular day rides from Ottawa.

The rail bed beneath your tires has a long history. The [Ottawa and Gatineau Valley Railway](https://www.gvhs.ca/gatineau/rail.html) was incorporated in 1871 by Gatineau Valley entrepreneurs including E.B. Eddy and Alonzo Wright, though trains didn't reach Wakefield until 1892. Canadian Pacific Railway leased the line in 1902 and ran freight and passenger service for decades. From 1992, the corridor found a second life as the [Hull–Chelsea–Wakefield tourist steam train](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hull%E2%80%93Chelsea%E2%80%93Wakefield_Railway) — a 1907 Swedish locomotive (No. 909) that drew roughly 50,000 visitors a year. Torrential rains in June 2011 caused landslides that damaged the tracks beyond repair, and the tourist train never ran again. The Municipality of Chelsea pulled up the rails in 2017 and the Voie Verte was born.

The old rail bed keeps the grade gentle the entire way. The Gatineau River is your companion for much of the ride, and the forest closes in on both sides. You'll pass through Cascades, Burnett, and Kirk's Ferry before reaching Chelsea village. In fall, the colours along this stretch are the kind that make you stop pedalling. Keep an eye out for the metal animal sculptures near Chemin Gleneagle — artist Yvan Xavier Regout made them from salvaged railway materials.

Chelsea is the natural halfway point, with food and a pub. Wakefield at the far end is worth the full ride — a village with character, good food, and the feeling of having properly gone somewhere.

There's a detour in effect as of 2024 due to construction on Chemin Riverside. Check conditions before heading out.
