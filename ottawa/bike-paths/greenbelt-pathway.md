Ottawa's Greenbelt is a 20,350-hectare ring of green space encircling the city — the [largest publicly owned greenbelt in the world](https://ncc-ccn.gc.ca/places/greenbelt). French planner [Jacques Gréber proposed it in 1950](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenbelt_(Ottawa)) as part of his master plan for the capital, and the federal government started acquiring land in 1956. Two trail systems run through it — one on the western side, one on the eastern — both part of the Trans Canada Trail.

Tires 32mm or wider are comfortable on both sections.

### Western trail

The western arm runs 21.2 kilometres through protected forest, wetlands, and farmland. The surface alternates between asphalt and stone dust, mostly flat with some hills. It stretches from Shirleys Bay past the Stony Swamp area — the largest stretch of forest in the Greenbelt, 20 square kilometres of protected wetland and woods. Meadows open up unexpectedly, creeks cross the path, and there are long stretches where the only sound is your tires on the gravel.

The western trail connects to the Watts Creek path, which links onward to the Kichi Zībī Mīkan along the Ottawa River.

### Eastern trail

The eastern arm runs 12.5 km through protected forest and wetland on fine gravel. Narrower than the western section, and connects to the [Ottawa River path](/paths/ottawa-river-pathway) at its northern end. Quieter and less travelled, with a wilder feel.

The [Mer Bleue Bog](https://ncc-ccn.gc.ca/places/mer-bleue) is the highlight of this section — a roughly 7,700-year-old peat bog and the largest in the National Capital Region. Scientists have been studying it for decades — it's [one of the most researched bogs in the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mer_Bleue_Bog), with a permanent station on site. Peat deposits reach up to 6 metres thick. The name means "blue sea" in French, from the fog that settles over the bog in the morning. A 1.2 km boardwalk trail leads into the bog itself — worth locking up the bike for.
